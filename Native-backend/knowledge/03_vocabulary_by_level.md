# English Vocabulary by Level — A1 to C1
## NATIVE 2.0 — Pedagogical Knowledge Base

---

## Overview

This document provides vocabulary guidance organized by CEFR level (A1-C1) for Spanish speakers learning English. NATIVE uses this to adapt conversation complexity, introduce new vocabulary at the right pace, and detect when a user is operating above or below their registered level.

---

## A1 — Beginner Level

### Core Vocabulary Areas
At A1, learners need approximately 500-700 words to function in basic interactions.

**Greetings and social phrases**
- Hello, Hi, Good morning, Good afternoon, Good evening, Goodbye, Bye
- How are you? / I'm fine, thank you / Nice to meet you
- Please, Thank you, You're welcome, Sorry, Excuse me

**Personal information**
- Name, age, country, city, job, family
- I am, My name is, I come from, I live in, I work as

**Numbers and time**
- Numbers 1-100, ordinal numbers
- Days of the week, months, seasons
- What time is it? / It's... o'clock

**Basic actions (high-frequency verbs)**
- be, have, do, go, come, want, need, like, eat, drink, sleep, work, study, speak, understand

**Common adjectives**
- big/small, good/bad, old/new, hot/cold, happy/sad, fast/slow, easy/difficult

**NATIVE conversation strategy at A1:**
- Maximum 5-6 words per sentence
- Use only high-frequency vocabulary
- Repeat key words in different contexts
- Accept any comprehensible English — do not over-correct

---

## A2 — Elementary Level

### Core Vocabulary Areas
At A2, learners have approximately 1,000-1,500 words.

**Daily life topics**
- Shopping: buy, sell, price, cheap, expensive, pay, receipt
- Food and restaurants: order, menu, delicious, hungry, thirsty
- Transport: bus, train, taxi, ticket, platform, departure, arrival
- Housing: apartment, rent, furniture, neighborhood, landlord

**Describing people and things**
- Physical appearance: tall, short, thin, heavy, blonde, dark, young, old
- Personality: friendly, shy, funny, serious, kind, rude, patient, nervous
- Comparing: bigger than, smaller than, the most, the least, as...as

**Common phrasal verbs (A2 level)**
- get up, wake up, put on, take off, turn on, turn off, look for, find out

**Common collocations**
- make a decision, do homework, have breakfast, take a photo, give advice

**NATIVE conversation strategy at A2:**
- Sentences of 8-10 words are appropriate
- Introduce 1-2 new vocabulary items per session naturally
- Echo new words back in different contexts to reinforce learning
- Focus corrections on errors that change meaning

---

## B1 — Intermediate Level

### Core Vocabulary Areas
At B1, learners have approximately 2,500-3,500 words.

**Abstract topics becoming accessible**
- Emotions and feelings: frustrated, relieved, disappointed, excited, anxious, proud
- Opinions and arguments: I think, I believe, In my opinion, On the other hand, However
- Problem and solution: issue, challenge, solution, approach, result, consequence

**Work and professional life**
- Career, position, salary, colleague, deadline, meeting, project, client, feedback
- Apply for a job, get promoted, resign, hire, fire, retire

**Travel and experiences**
- Adventure, experience, destination, culture, tradition, recommend, worth visiting
- I've been to, I've never, Have you ever...?

**Common idioms at B1 level**
- break a leg (good luck)
- hit the nail on the head (be exactly right)
- under the weather (feeling ill)
- cost an arm and a leg (very expensive)
- piece of cake (very easy)

**Phrasal verbs (B1 level)**
- carry on, give up, look forward to, run out of, set up, bring up, come across

**NATIVE conversation strategy at B1:**
- Natural conversation flow — do not simplify unnecessarily
- Introduce idioms naturally and gauge comprehension from response
- Correct grammar errors that don't impede communication only if repeated
- Begin pushing vocabulary boundaries gently

---

## B2 — Upper Intermediate Level

### Core Vocabulary Areas
At B2, learners have approximately 5,000-7,000 words.

**Nuanced expression**
- Instead of "very good" → excellent, outstanding, remarkable, impressive
- Instead of "very bad" → terrible, dreadful, appalling, dire
- Instead of "happy" → thrilled, delighted, elated, overjoyed
- Instead of "sad" → devastated, heartbroken, despondent, melancholic

**Academic and professional vocabulary**
- analyze, evaluate, assess, implement, demonstrate, elaborate, clarify, justify
- hypothesis, evidence, argument, perspective, framework, methodology

**Discourse markers for sophistication**
- Furthermore, Moreover, Nevertheless, Nonetheless, In contrast, Consequently
- As a result, It follows that, This suggests that, It could be argued that

**Formal vs informal register awareness**
- Formal: I would like to inquire / Informal: I want to ask
- Formal: I regret to inform you / Informal: I'm sorry to tell you
- Formal: in accordance with / Informal: following

**Advanced idioms**
- beat around the bush (avoid the main topic)
- bite off more than you can chew (take on too much)
- burn the midnight oil (work very late)
- once in a blue moon (very rarely)
- the ball is in your court (it's your decision now)

**NATIVE conversation strategy at B2:**
- Use rich, varied vocabulary naturally
- Push the user to use more sophisticated expressions
- Correct subtle errors and stylistic choices gently
- Engage with complex topics, abstract ideas, hypotheticals

---

## C1 — Advanced Level

### Core Vocabulary Areas
At C1, learners have approximately 10,000+ words.

**Sophisticated vocabulary**
- Nuance between similar words: affect/impact/influence, use/employ/utilize, get/obtain/acquire
- Formal writing vocabulary: albeit, hitherto, notwithstanding, pursuant to
- Sophisticated verbs: scrutinize, epitomize, corroborate, exacerbate, mitigate

**Idiomatic competence**
- Native-level idiom use without over-reliance
- Understanding of cultural references
- Code-switching between formal and informal registers seamlessly

**Collocations at advanced level**
- reach a consensus, raise awareness, draw a conclusion, pose a question
- bridge the gap, come to terms with, take something for granted

**NATIVE conversation strategy at C1:**
- Treat as near-native — full natural conversation
- Only note very subtle errors or unusual collocations
- Engage as intellectual equal — debates, nuanced opinions, complex narratives
- Push toward native-like fluency and natural expression

## Vocabulary Introduction Strategy for NATIVE

When introducing new vocabulary during conversation:

1. **Use the word naturally first** — let the user encounter it in context
2. **Check comprehension indirectly** — does their response show they understood?
3. **If uncertain** — the character can define it naturally: "Oh, I mean..."
4. **Never stop conversation to teach vocabulary** — integration is the method
5. **Spaced repetition** — use the same word 2-3 times in different contexts across the session

## False Friends — High Priority for Spanish Speakers

These words look similar in Spanish but mean something different in English:

| English Word | Looks like Spanish | Spanish Meaning | English Meaning |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| embarrassed | embarazada | pregnant | feeling ashamed |
| sensible | sensible | sensitive | reasonable/practical |
| actually | actualmente | currently | in fact/really |
| eventually | eventualmente | possibly | in the end |
| library | librería | bookshop | place to borrow books |
| fabric | fábrica | factory | cloth/material |
| assist | asistir | to attend | to help |
| exit | éxito | success | way out |
