# English Pronunciation Guide for Spanish Speakers
## NATIVE 2.0 — Pedagogical Knowledge Base

---

## Overview

Spanish is a phonetic language — every letter sounds the same every time. English is not. The same letter combination can produce multiple sounds, and many English sounds simply do not exist in Spanish. This creates systematic pronunciation challenges for Spanish speakers that NATIVE must identify and address.

---

## 1. Vowel Sounds — The Core Challenge

Spanish has 5 vowel sounds. English has approximately 20. This is the root of most pronunciation difficulties.

### Short vs Long Vowels
English distinguishes between short and long versions of vowels. Spanish does not.

| Minimal Pair | Short Sound | Long Sound | Common Error |
|-------------|-------------|------------|--------------|
| bit / beat | /ɪ/ | /iː/ | Spanish speakers pronounce both as "ee" |
| full / fool | /ʊ/ | /uː/ | Both pronounced as "oo" |
| hot / hope | /ɒ/ | /oʊ/ | Difficulty with the diphthong |
| cut / cart | /ʌ/ | /ɑː/ | Both pronounced as "a" |

### The Schwa /ə/ — Most Common Sound in English
The schwa is the reduced vowel sound in unstressed syllables. It does not exist in Spanish.

Examples:
- **about** → /əˈbaʊt/ (first syllable is schwa)
- **teacher** → /ˈtiːtʃər/ (last syllable is schwa)
- **family** → /ˈfæməli/ (second syllable is schwa)

**Error pattern:** Spanish speakers tend to pronounce every syllable with full vowel quality, making speech sound unnatural.

## 2. Consonant Sounds That Don't Exist in Spanish

### The TH Sound /θ/ and /ð/
This is one of the most recognizable Spanish-speaker errors.

| Sound | Symbol | Examples | Common Substitution |
|-------|--------|----------|---------------------|
| Unvoiced TH | /θ/ | think, three, bath, month | Replaced with /s/ or /t/ |
| Voiced TH | /ð/ | the, this, that, brother | Replaced with /d/ or /z/ |

**Practice pairs:**
- think → sink (common confusion)
- three → tree (common confusion)
- the → de (common confusion)
- this → dis (common confusion)

**Correction approach:** "The sound starts with your tongue between your teeth — try it slowly."

---

### The V Sound /v/
Spanish does not distinguish between /b/ and /v/. Both letters produce a similar bilabial sound.

| Error | Correct |
|-------|---------|
| "berry good" | "very good" |
| "I beliebe it" | "I believe it" |
| "bideo" | "video" |

**Practice words:** very, voice, video, love, live, have, believe, involve

---

### The W Sound /w/
The English /w/ does not exist in Spanish. Spanish speakers often substitute it with /gw/ or /v/.

| Error | Correct |
|-------|---------|
| "güater" | "water" |
| "güork" | "work" |

**Practice words:** water, work, word, world, would, want, woman, wonder

---

### Final Consonants — Syllable-Final Sounds
Spanish rarely ends syllables with consonant clusters. English does it constantly.

**Common omissions:**
- "firs" instead of "first"
- "nex" instead of "next"
- "tes" instead of "test"
- "lis" instead of "list"

**This is critical for communication** because omitting final consonants often changes word meaning.

---

## 3. Consonant Clusters

English frequently clusters multiple consonants at the start or end of words. Spanish almost never does.

**Common errors — adding a vowel:**
- "es-tream" instead of "stream"
- "es-tudent" instead of "student" (directly from Spanish "estudiante")
- "es-peak" instead of "speak"

**Common errors — dropping consonants:**
- "aks" instead of "asks"
- "sixs" instead of "sixth"

---

## 4. Word Stress — The Rhythm of English

English is a stress-timed language. Spanish is syllable-timed. This creates a fundamentally different rhythm.

In English:
- Stressed syllables are longer, louder, and higher pitched
- Unstressed syllables are reduced (often to schwa)
- Word stress can change meaning

**Stress pairs where meaning changes:**
| Word | As Noun | As Verb |
|------|---------|---------|
| record | RE-cord | re-CORD |
| present | PRE-sent | pre-SENT |
| permit | PER-mit | per-MIT |
| protest | PRO-test | pro-TEST |

---

## 5. Intonation Patterns

### Questions
- Yes/No questions: rising intonation at the end ↗
- Wh- questions: falling intonation at the end ↘
- Tag questions: rising if uncertain ↗, falling if confirming ↘

### Statements
- Declarative statements: falling intonation ↘
- Lists: rising on each item until the last ↗↗↗↘

**Spanish speakers error:** Using flat intonation throughout, making speech sound robotic or rude.

---

## 6. Pronunciation Scoring Guide for NATIVE

When Pronunciation Assessment scores are received, interpret them as follows:

| Score Range | Interpretation | NATIVE Response |
|------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 90-100 | Excellent — near native | No correction needed |
| 75-89 | Good — minor issues | Note only if the same word is mispronounced again |
| 60-74 | Acceptable — noticeable accent | Gentle echo of correct pronunciation |
| 40-59 | Needs work — communication affected | Natural correction integrated in response |
| 0-39 | Significant issue — meaning unclear | Direct pronunciation practice suggested |

---

## 7. Priority Sounds for Spanish Speakers (by frequency of error)

1. TH sounds (/θ/ and /ð/) — very high frequency error
2. V vs B distinction — high frequency error  
3. Short vowels (/ɪ/, /ʊ/, /æ/, /ʌ/) — high frequency error
4. Final consonants — high frequency error
5. Schwa reduction — medium frequency error
6. Word stress patterns — medium frequency error
7. W sound — medium frequency error
8. Consonant clusters — low-medium frequency error

---

## Pedagogical Note for NATIVE Agent

Pronunciation correction should feel like a native speaker naturally helping a friend — not a phonetics lesson. When a score below 60 is detected on a specific word, the agent should either:
1. Use the word correctly in the next response so the user hears it
2. Ask the user to repeat naturally: "Sorry, did you say [correct word]? Say it again for me"
3. Only provide explicit phonetic guidance for words scoring below 40 or repeated errors
